Artifact 01
Building an AI-Powered Bakery Customer Assistant
AI Lab Case Study
ChatGPT Custom GPT STORM AI Chatbase
Introduction

I'm a graduate student in Indiana Wesleyan University. This artifact documents my work in the course's AI Lab, where I explored multiple generative AI tools and then applied a design-thinking process to plan, build, and test a functional AI chatbot.

Description

This artifact captures a multi-stage exploration of generative AI: comparing outputs across repeated LLM prompts, holding a multi-turn conversation with a custom GPT, evaluating a research-assistant tool (STORM AI) for source-backed writing, and finally planning and deploying a working AI chatbot for a bakery customer-service use case.

Objective

To build practical fluency with different categories of generative AI tools, and to apply a design-thinking framework (empathy, problem definition, ideation, prototyping, testing) to go from a concept to a deployed, working AI product.

Process

I began by running an identical structured prompt across three separate LLM chats to compare consistency and constraint-following. I then had an extended conversation with a custom GPT, deliberately working through six interaction types (requesting changes, adding context, asking for improvements, probing its reasoning, posing hypotheticals, and requesting a summary) to test how well it retained instructions. Next, I used STORM AI to generate a sourced research article, examining its self-directed research process and noting where it explicitly declined to answer due to insufficient sources rather than guessing. Finally, I answered ten design-thinking planning questions before building the assistant in Chatbase, reviewing its privacy policy and terms of service, and running five planned test scenarios against the deployed bot identifying and documenting where its actual behavior diverged from my plan.

Tools & Tech

ChatGPT (general LLM), a custom GPT (Master Chef Cooking, Food & Recipes, Nutrition, Diet), STORM AI (research assistant), Chatbase (no-code AI agent builder).

Value Proposition

Unique value: this artifact shows I can move beyond casually prompting an AI tool to systematically evaluating it checking its math, catching constraint violations, and noticing when its actual behavior deviates from a written plan, rather than assuming the first output is correct.

Relevance: for an evaluator assessing applied AI/ML competency, this demonstrates the full arc from conceptual planning to a deployed, tested artifact, along with the critical judgment to identify and reason about gaps rather than just report success.

Artifact 02
Types of Machine Learning (Group Presentation)
Group Presentation
PowerPoint Group Collaboration In-Class Presentation
Introduction

This artifact documents a group presentation I delivered on campus with three teammates. Our topic was the four core types of machine learning, and the goal was to break down a technical subject into something a non-expert audience could actually follow and engage with.

Description

As a group of four, we built and delivered a 16-slide presentation covering supervised, unsupervised, reinforcement, and self/semi-supervised learning. Rather than just defining each type, we grounded every concept in an everyday analogy (studying with an answer key, sorting Lego by color, training a puppy with treats, fill-in-the-blank practice), walked through a real case study on an AI system used to detect eye disease, and closed with an open discussion on accountability and ethics in ML.

Objective

To take a technical ML concept and make it accessible to a mixed audience, while practicing how to present as a team, handle audience questions in real time, and connect classroom theory to real-world use and consequences.

Process

My teammates and I split the four ML types across the group, then worked together on the shared framing (the "everyday example" structure) so the presentation felt consistent instead of four disconnected sections stitched together. I focused on translating the more abstract material reinforcement learning and the ethics section into plain language and researched the case study we used to ground the ethics discussion in something concrete instead of hypothetical. We rehearsed as a group beforehand, which is where we caught that our transition between conceptual foundation and the technical breakdown moved too fast for a first-time audience.

Tools & Tech

PowerPoint, group collaboration/rehearsal, in-person presentation delivery, audience Q&A.

Value Proposition

Unique value: this artifact shows a different skill set than my chatbot project communicating a technical concept clearly to a live audience, collaborating as part of a team, and adapting in real time to questions, rather than iterating alone against a written test plan.

Relevance: for an evaluator assessing applied AI/ML competency, this demonstrates that I can explain foundational ML concepts in an accessible way and work effectively within a team, which matters just as much as the technical building skills shown in Artifact 1.

Customization for the Audience

Since our classmates weren't all coming in with the same technical background, we leaned hard on plain-language analogies instead of jargon, and used the eye-disease case study to make the ethics section feel real rather than abstract. We deliberately kept a summary table near the end so people who lost the thread on any one type could still walk away with the big picture.

Lessons Learned

Presenting this taught me that understanding a concept well enough to explain it out loud is a different skill than understanding it well enough to answer a quiz question. When a classmate asked why self-supervised learning needs so much data compared to supervised learning, I realized my own explanation had been a little fuzzy on that point until I had to defend it live. Group presenting also meant learning to hand off cleanly between sections without losing the audience's attention in the gaps.

Feedback & Revisions

The clearest feedback came from our own rehearsal: we noticed our jump from the "what is ML" intro into the four technical types felt abrupt, so we added a short bridging slide to slow that transition down. During the actual presentation, a couple of the discussion questions we posed at the end (like who's responsible when an AI makes a mistake) sparked more debate than we expected, which told us that section landed the way we wanted even though it wasn't something we could fully plan for in advance.
